# Timeless Copywriting Principles - Part 1: Foundation & Theory

**For LLM Reference: Universal principles that transcend platforms, industries, and formats**

---

## Document Purpose & Usage

This document contains the **philosophical foundation and timeless principles** of effective copywriting, distilled from the masters: Ogilvy, Bernbach, Hopkins, Caples, Schwartz, and Burnett.

**When to use this document:**
- Before creating any copy (ads, landing pages, emails)
- When copy isn't performing (return to fundamentals)
- When evaluating quality (use as scoring rubric)
- When teaching or learning copywriting craft

**What this document does NOT contain:**
- Platform-specific tactics (see Digital Creative docs)
- Landing page structures (see Landing Page docs)
- Templates and examples (see Part 2 of each set)

**Core Philosophy:**
> "The work is in what you removed. Respect the reader's time. Make one clear promise. Talk to self-interest."

## Part 1: The Foundational Mindset

### 1.1 The Standards You Must Hold

Before learning techniques, internalize these standards:

**Standard 1: Respect Reader Attention as Sacred**
- Every reader gives you seconds, not minutes
- Their attention is a gift, not a right
- Waste no words, waste no time
- If you cannot say it clearly, you do not understand it yet

**Standard 2: Serve Self-Interest, Not Your Ego**
- Readers care about their problems, not your cleverness
- Turn "we do X" into "you get Y"
- Features describe you; benefits describe them
- Emotional outcomes > functional features

**Standard 3: Clarity Is Your First Duty**
- Confusion = instant abandonment
- Clever is fine ONLY if instantly clear
- Default to simple, concrete language
- If a 12-year-old cannot understand it, simplify

**Standard 4: One Idea Wins, Multiple Ideas Lose**
- Every piece of copy has ONE job
- Multiple promises dilute power
- Side benefits belong in different assets
- Decide the single most important thing, then cut everything else

**Standard 5: Specificity Beats Vagueness**
- "Quality" is meaningless; "twice as fast" is not
- Numbers, names, and comparisons build belief
- Generic claims sound like lies
- Concrete details feel true

### 1.2 The Constraint Advantage

**Constraints sharpen thinking. They do not limit creativity—they force clarity.**

**Core Constraints:**

1. **Word Count Limits**
   - Billboard: 3-7 words
   - Digital ad headline: 30-70 characters (depending on platform)
   - Landing page headline: 8-15 words
   - Email subject line: 30-50 characters

2. **Attention Span Limits**
   - Billboard: 1-3 seconds (glance from moving car)
   - Social ad: 1-5 seconds (scroll mode)
   - Landing page hero: 5-10 seconds (scan for relevance)
   - Email: 3 seconds (subject line + preview)

3. **Cognitive Load Limits**
   - One idea per asset
   - One emotion per message
   - One action per ask
   - Maximum 3-5 benefit points per page

**How to Use Constraints:**
- Write long first (explore all ideas)
- Cut to half (remove padding)
- Cut to half again (force prioritization)
- Test: Can you say it in one breath? One glance? One thought?

**The "One Breath Test":**
Read your line aloud. If you need to pause mid-sentence or gasp for air, it's too long. A billboard line should finish in one natural breath. This forces brevity AND natural rhythm.

**The "Glance Test":**
Show the copy to someone for 2-3 seconds, then hide it. Can they repeat the core message? If not, simplify.

## Part 2: The Eight Timeless Principles

These principles apply to ALL copywriting, across every format and platform.

### Principle 1: Clarity Beats Cleverness

**The Rule:**
When time is short or stakes are high, always choose the clearer option over the clever one.

**Why It Matters:**
- Clever wordplay requires cognitive processing time
- Confusion = abandonment
- Your goal is communication, not admiration
- Cleverness that delays comprehension kills conversion

**When Cleverness Works:**
- It must be **instantly** clear (no "wait, I get it!" moment)
- The twist adds meaning, not just entertainment
- Brand voice demands playfulness
- Low-stakes awareness content

**When Clarity Must Win:**
- High-intent moments (search ads, checkout pages)
- Professional contexts (B2B, LinkedIn)
- Complex or new products
- Cold audiences who don't know you yet

**The Clarity Test:**
1. Remove all wordplay and puns
2. Does the line still work? If yes, you probably don't need the clever version.
3. If the clever version tests significantly better, use it. Otherwise, default to clear.

**Philosophical Grounding (Hopkins):**
> "Fine writing is a distinct disadvantage. So is unique literary style. They take attention from the subject."
> — Claude Hopkins, *Scientific Advertising*

**Philosophical Grounding (Ogilvy):**
> "If it doesn't sell, it isn't creative."
> — David Ogilvy

### Principle 2: One Strong Promise Per Asset

**The Rule:**
Every ad, page, or email should make ONE clear promise. Not two. Not three. One.

**Why It Matters:**
- The human brain processes one idea at a time under time pressure
- Multiple promises compete for attention
- Each additional benefit dilutes the power of the primary one
- Decision paralysis increases with option count

**How to Identify the ONE Promise:**
1. List all possible benefits
2. For each, ask: "If I could only say ONE thing, would this be it?"
3. Choose the benefit with the strongest emotional pull for your target audience
4. Relegate other benefits to supporting copy or different assets

**What "One Promise" Does NOT Mean:**
- ❌ You can never mention other benefits
- ❌ Landing pages can only have one sentence
- ❌ All features must be hidden

**What "One Promise" DOES Mean:**
- ✓ The MAIN message is singular and dominant
- ✓ Supporting points reinforce the core promise
- ✓ The reader can summarize your message in one sentence
- ✓ You know exactly what job this asset has to do

**The Ogilvy Test:**
After reading your copy, ask: "What is this ad selling?" If the answer is unclear or multi-part, you have too many promises.

**Philosophical Grounding (Ogilvy):**
> "You cannot bore people into buying your product. You can only interest them in buying it."
> — David Ogilvy

The fastest way to bore someone? Try to tell them everything at once.

### Principle 3: Serve Self-Interest First

**The Rule:**
Lead with what the reader gains, not what you do or what your product is.

**Why It Matters:**
- Readers scan for relevance to THEIR life
- "We are X" triggers "so what?" response
- "You get Y" triggers "tell me more" response
- Self-interest is the universal attention trigger

**The Translation Formula:**

```
FEATURE (what it is)
  ↓
FUNCTION (what it does)
  ↓
BENEFIT (what you get)
  ↓
OUTCOME (how life changes)
```

**Example:**
- Feature: "AI transcription"
- Function: "Converts speech to text automatically"
- Benefit: "Never take notes in meetings"
- Outcome: "Be fully present with your team"

**Use Outcome-level language whenever possible.**

**Self-Interest Language Markers:**
- "You" (not "we")
- "Your" (not "our")
- Results, not processes
- Feelings, not features
- Time saved, stress removed, status gained

**The Hopkins Principle:**
> "People buy products for what they will do for them, not for what they are."
> — Claude Hopkins

### Principle 4: Be Concrete & Specific

**The Rule:**
Use numbers, names, comparisons, and real-world details instead of vague quality claims.

**Why It Matters:**
- Vague claims trigger skepticism ("they all say that")
- Specific details feel true, even if not yet believed
- The brain processes concrete language faster
- Specificity signals confidence and evidence

**Specificity Hierarchy:**

```
Level 0 (Vague):        "Better results"
Level 1 (Category):     "More sales"
Level 2 (Quantified):   "30% more sales"
Level 3 (Contextualized): "30% more sales in 90 days"
Level 4 (Full Context): "From $10K to $13K MRR in Q1"
```

**Always aim for Level 3 minimum. Level 4 when you have proof.**

**Where to Add Specificity:**
- Time frames: "in 6 weeks" not "quickly"
- Quantities: "2,400 students" not "thousands"
- Comparisons: "twice as fast" not "faster"
- Real examples: "exit 12" not "nearby"
- Actual numbers: "$1,499" not "affordable"

**The Schwartz Principle:**
> "The power of a specific fact is inversely proportional to the number of similar facts presented."
> — Eugene Schwartz

One specific, vivid detail beats ten generic claims.

**Banned Vague Words:**
- Quality, excellence, innovative, revolutionary
- World-class, best-in-class, cutting-edge
- Solutions, robust, seamless, synergy
- Transform, empower, enable, leverage

Replace with: numbers, names, comparisons, outcomes

### Principle 5: Use Strong Verbs & Simple Words

**The Rule:**
Choose active verbs and everyday language over weak verbs and fancy vocabulary.

**Why It Matters:**
- Strong verbs create motion and energy
- Simple words process faster
- Fancy language signals insecurity
- The best copy sounds like natural speech

**Strong vs Weak Verbs:**

```
WEAK               STRONG
Improve         →  Fix, sharpen, boost
Change          →  Transform, shift, flip
Help            →  Save, protect, guide
Provide         →  Give, deliver, hand
Enable          →  Unlock, open, free
Facilitate      →  Speed, smooth, ease
Optimize        →  Tune, tighten, hone
```

**The Verb Strength Test:**
Replace your verb with "do" or "make." If the sentence still makes sense, your verb is too weak.

Example:
- "Facilitate better outcomes" → "Do better outcomes" (makes no sense = weak verb)
- "Win more deals" → still clear = strong verb

**Simple Word Principle:**
If there's a shorter, more common word that means the same thing, use it.

```
FANCY              SIMPLE
Utilize         →  Use
Purchase        →  Buy
Commence        →  Start
Terminate       →  End
Leverage        →  Use
Optimized       →  Better
```

**The Hemingway Standard:**
> "Use short words, short sentences, short paragraphs."
> — Ernest Hemingway

**The 12-Year-Old Test:**
Could a 12-year-old understand every word without a dictionary? If not, simplify. This doesn't mean dumbing down—it means respecting clarity.

### Principle 6: Create Contrast & Surprise

**The Rule:**
Use contrast and pattern interrupts to create stopping power and memorability.

**Why It Matters:**
- Contrast creates visual and cognitive interest
- Surprise breaks scroll patterns
- Expected copy gets ignored
- The brain pays attention to novelty

**Types of Contrast:**

**1. State Contrast (Before/After)**
```
Structure: From [bad state] to [good state]
Example: "Tired at noon? Alert till closing."
```

**2. Speed Contrast**
```
Structure: [Slow old way] → [Fast new way]
Example: "5 hours of research → 5 minutes with AI"
```

**3. Effort Contrast**
```
Structure: [Hard way] vs [Easy way]
Example: "Skip the manual work. Automate in clicks."
```

**4. Expectation Reversal**
```
Structure: [Expected belief] → [Unexpected truth]
Example: "Don't work harder. Work on 3 things only."
```

**Pattern Interrupt Techniques:**
- Lead with a "No" or "Stop" (unexpected)
- Ask a question they expect "yes" to (then pivot)
- Make a claim that sounds wrong (then prove it)
- Use brevity where others use length

**The Bernbach Philosophy:**
> "If your advertising goes unnoticed, everything else is academic."
> — Bill Bernbach

Contrast and surprise are how you get noticed.

**When to Use Surprise:**
- Cold audiences (awareness stage)
- High-saturation platforms (social feeds)
- Competitive markets (many similar options)

**When to Use Straightforward Clarity:**
- Hot audiences (high intent, ready to buy)
- Complex products (reduce cognitive load)
- Professional contexts (credibility > creativity)

### Principle 7: Visual-Copy Partnership

**The Rule:**
Copy and visuals should work together, not repeat each other.

**Why It Matters:**
- Repetition wastes limited space and attention
- Good copy adds meaning the visual cannot convey
- Visual shows, copy tells WHY or WHAT'S NEXT
- Best ads have 1+1=3 synergy

**The Partnership Principles:**

**1. Don't Repeat the Obvious**
```
BAD:
Visual: Person drinking coffee
Copy: "Drink our coffee"

GOOD:
Visual: Person drinking coffee
Copy: "Awake by exit 12"
```

**2. Add Meaning, Not Description**
```
BAD:
Visual: Phone showing app interface
Copy: "Our mobile app"

GOOD:
Visual: Phone showing app interface
Copy: "Track every mile, every calorie, every day"
```

**3. Create Curiosity Loops**
```
Visual: Surprising image (e.g., person sleeping at desk)
Copy: "This is your brain before Northwind Coffee"
```

**What Visuals Do Best:**
- Show the product or outcome
- Create emotional tone
- Demonstrate scale or comparison
- Grab attention through color/composition

**What Copy Does Best:**
- Explain the benefit or promise
- Add specificity (numbers, timeframes)
- Create urgency or FOMO
- Guide to action (CTA)

**The Collaboration Test:**
Cover the copy. Does the visual alone communicate something?
Cover the visual. Does the copy alone make sense?
If both pass, check: Does combining them create MORE impact than either alone?

### Principle 8: Make the Brand Clear

**The Rule:**
Your audience must know WHO is speaking without effort.

**Why It Matters:**
- A great message for an unknown brand = wasted money
- Brand recall drives long-term value
- Clear attribution builds trust
- People buy from brands they recognize

**Brand Clarity Checklist:**
□ Logo is large enough to read at glance
□ Brand name appears in natural places (not forced)
□ Tone and voice are consistent with brand
□ Visual style matches brand guidelines
□ Tagline or positioning is recognizable

**When to Include Brand Name in Copy:**
- New or unknown brands (build awareness)
- Competitive markets (differentiate clearly)
- Premium positioning (leverage brand equity)
- Natural fit in sentence structure

**When Logo Alone Is Enough:**
- Established brands (Nike, Apple, Coca-Cola)
- Visual-first formats (Instagram, Pinterest)
- When copy space is extremely limited

**The Schwartz Test:**
Show the ad with brand name/logo removed. Can someone guess whose ad it is? If yes, your brand voice is strong. If no, you may be generic.

## Part 3: Quality Assessment Framework

### 3.1 The Universal Quality Rubric

Use this to score ANY piece of copy (ads, headlines, pages) on a 1-5 scale.

| Criterion | 1 – Weak | 3 – Acceptable | 5 – Excellent |
|-----------|----------|----------------|---------------|
| **Clarity** | Confusing on first read; jargon or muddled idea | Understandable but slightly slow or fuzzy | Instantly clear at a glance; no confusion |
| **Brevity** | Long and wordy; could lose 50%+ | Acceptable length but somewhat padded | Lean and tight; every word pulls weight |
| **Benefit** | Pure feature description; no clear payoff | Benefit implied but not explicit | Clear "what I get" front and center |
| **Emotional Impact** | Flat; no feeling; pure logic | Mild interest or concern | Clear emotion: relief, desire, pride, urgency |
| **Specificity** | Vague claims ("quality," "best") | Some numbers or details | Concrete: numbers, names, comparisons |
| **Brand Fit** | Tone clashes with brand voice | Roughly on brand, not sharp | Feels exactly like this brand would say |
| **Memorability** | Generic; could be anyone | Somewhat distinctive | Unique and easy to recall or repeat |

**Scoring Guidelines:**
- **1-2**: Needs major rewrite
- **3**: Functional but not compelling; iterate
- **4**: Good; ship if time-constrained
- **5**: Excellent; this is the standard

**Aim for 4+ on every criterion before finalizing.**

### 3.2 The Pre-Flight Checklist

Run every piece of copy through these gates:

**Gate 1: Comprehension**
□ Can it be understood in ONE read?
□ Would a 12-year-old understand every word?
□ Is the main idea clear in 5 seconds or less?

**Gate 2: Relevance**
□ Does it lead with reader self-interest?
□ Is the benefit obvious?
□ Would the target audience care?

**Gate 3: Simplicity**
□ Is there ONE main idea (not 2-3)?
□ Are all words simple and conversational?
□ Could you cut 20% without losing meaning?

**Gate 4: Specificity**
□ Are there numbers, names, or comparisons?
□ Is it more specific than competitors?
□ Do claims feel credible and concrete?

**Gate 5: Emotion**
□ Does it trigger a clear emotion?
□ Is the tone appropriate for context?
□ Would this move someone to act?

**Gate 6: Brand**
□ Is the brand clearly identifiable?
□ Does tone match brand voice?
□ Would existing customers recognize this?

**If ANY gate fails, revise before shipping.**

### 3.3 The Comparative Quality Test

**How to Use:**
1. Place your copy next to 3 competitor pieces
2. Remove all brand names
3. Ask: Which is clearest? Most specific? Most compelling?
4. If yours doesn't win on at least 2/3 criteria, rewrite

**Competitive Advantage Questions:**
- Is your benefit more specific than theirs?
- Is your claim more credible?
- Is your tone more authentic?
- Would YOU click yours over theirs?

## Part 4: The Rewriting Process

### 4.1 The Three-Pass Method

**Pass 1: Expand**
- Write everything that could be said
- Explore all angles and benefits
- Don't worry about length
- Goal: Capture all possibilities

**Pass 2: Cut to Core**
- Remove 50% of words
- Delete setup phrases, fluff, repetition
- Keep only essential meaning
- Goal: Clarity through brevity

**Pass 3: Sharpen**
- Replace weak verbs with strong ones
- Add specificity (numbers, names)
- Inject emotion and tone
- Goal: Maximum impact per word

**Example:**

```
Pass 1 (Expanded):
"Our revolutionary AI-powered platform provides comprehensive solutions that help marketing professionals optimize their workflow processes and achieve better results through cutting-edge automation technology and intelligent assistance capabilities."

Pass 2 (Cut to Core):
"AI platform that helps marketers work faster through automation."

Pass 3 (Sharpened):
"AI that cuts marketing work by half."
```

### 4.2 Word-Level Revision Tactics

**Tactic 1: Remove Setup Words**
Delete: "that," "really," "very," "just," "actually," "in order to"

```
Before: "We really help you to actually save time"
After: "We save you time"
```

**Tactic 2: Replace Weak Verb + Adverb with Strong Verb**
```
"Quickly improve" → "Accelerate"
"Completely change" → "Transform"
"Greatly enhance" → "Amplify"
```

**Tactic 3: Convert Passive to Active Voice**
```
Passive: "Leads are generated by our system"
Active: "Our system generates leads"
Better: "Generate 3x more leads"
```

**Tactic 4: Front-Load Benefits**
```
Weak: "With our AI, you can write better content faster"
Strong: "Write better content, 10x faster with AI"
```

### 4.3 The "One Breath" Refinement

**Process:**
1. Read your line aloud
2. Note where you pause or rush
3. If you can't say it in one breath, it's too long
4. Cut until you can speak it naturally without gasping

**This forces:**
- Natural rhythm
- Conversational tone
- Appropriate length

## Part 5: Philosophical Principles from the Masters

### 5.1 Hopkins: Scientific Advertising

**Core Teaching:**
Advertising is salesmanship in print. Test everything. Measure everything.

**Key Principles:**
1. **Specificity Sells:** "Platitudes and generalities roll off the human understanding like water from a duck."
2. **Reason Why:** Give people a reason to believe your claim
3. **Service, Not Ego:** "The only purpose of advertising is to make sales."
4. **Test Before Scale:** "Almost any question can be answered, cheaply, quickly and finally, by a test campaign."

**Application:**
- Ban vague claims
- Provide evidence or reason
- Focus on sales outcomes, not awards
- A/B test before committing budget

### 5.2 Ogilvy: Respect the Consumer

**Core Teaching:**
"The consumer isn't a moron; she is your wife."

**Key Principles:**
1. **Do Your Homework:** Research before writing
2. **Long Copy Sells:** When people are interested, they read
3. **Headlines Matter Most:** 5x more people read headlines than body copy
4. **Brand Building:** Every ad should contribute to brand image

**Application:**
- Never condescend to your audience
- Don't fear long copy when selling complex things
- Invest maximum effort in headlines
- Maintain consistent brand voice

### 5.3 Bernbach: Creative Revolution

**Core Teaching:**
"If your advertising goes unnoticed, everything else is academic."

**Key Principles:**
1. **Be Different:** Don't copy competitors
2. **Truth Well Told:** Find the unique truth about your product
3. **Art + Copy Partnership:** Great creative needs both disciplines
4. **Persuasion Through Delight:** People remember what entertains them

**Application:**
- Pattern interrupt and surprise
- Find your unique angle
- Invest in visual-copy synergy
- Make ads people want to share

### 5.4 Caples: Proven Direct Response

**Core Teaching:**
"The advertiser who can find a good idea can make a fortune."

**Key Principles:**
1. **Headlines Sell:** 75% of success is the headline
2. **Offer Matters:** What you offer > how you say it
3. **Test Scientifically:** Track everything, trust data
4. **Curiosity Hooks:** Make them want to know more

**Application:**
- Write 20+ headlines, test the best
- Focus on offer strength
- Build testing into process
- Use curiosity gaps strategically

### 5.5 Schwartz: Awareness Stages

**Core Teaching:**
"The power of your headline is directly proportional to how much your prospect already wants your product."

**Key Principles:**
1. **Match Awareness Level:** Different stages need different copy
2. **Most Aware:** Name the product and offer
3. **Problem Aware:** Name the problem and solution
4. **Unaware:** Create curiosity, not direct pitch
5. **Sophistication Matters:** What's been said before changes what works now

**Application:**
- Identify audience awareness level first
- High-intent: direct, specific, offer-focused
- Low-intent: problem-focused, curiosity-driven
- Competitive markets: find new angle or proof

## Part 6: Edge Cases & Adaptations

### 6.1 Complex or B2B Products

**The Challenge:**
You cannot explain a full enterprise platform in short copy.

**The Solution:**
Focus on the highest-level outcome for the buyer, not the product features.

**Principles:**
- Talk outcomes, not architecture
- Use industry language (not jargon)
- Focus on business impact (time, money, risk)
- Leverage social proof (logos, case studies)

**Example:**
Don't say: "Multi-tenant cloud infrastructure with API-first architecture"
Do say: "Close deals 40% faster with CRM your team will actually use"

### 6.2 Regulated Categories (Finance, Healthcare, Legal)

**The Challenge:**
Claims are restricted; guarantees are forbidden.

**The Solution:**
Focus on access, simplicity, guidance, and feelings instead of hard outcomes.

**Principles:**
- Emphasize process over promises
- Highlight credentials and trust signals
- Use softer language ("help," "support," "guide")
- Focus on risk reduction, not gain maximization

**Example:**
Don't say: "Guaranteed 15% returns on investment"
Do say: "Fiduciary advisors who put your goals first"

### 6.3 Luxury or Minimal Brands

**The Challenge:**
Premium positioning requires restraint and understatement.

**The Solution:**
Use fewer words, more space, loaded meaning.

**Principles:**
- Brevity signals confidence
- Let design carry weight
- Imply rather than state
- Focus on craftsmanship and heritage

**Example:**
Mass market: "Affordable luxury watches for everyone"
Luxury: "Time, sharpened." (with image of watch)

### 6.4 High-Saturation Markets

**The Challenge:**
Everyone sounds the same; cutting through noise is hard.

**The Solution:**
Extreme specificity, contrarian positioning, or founder voice.

**Principles:**
- Avoid category language everyone uses
- Find the opposite of what competitors say
- Use ultra-specific niche positioning
- Leverage personal story or POV

**Example:**
Generic SaaS: "Project management for modern teams"
Differentiated: "Project management for people who hate project management"

## Part 7: Standards & Excellence

### 7.1 The Classic Ad Standard

**The Question:**
Could you pin your work next to classic ads from Ogilvy, Bernbach, or Caples without embarrassment?

**How to Raise Your Standard:**
1. Study classic ads (see reference list in Part 2)
2. Notice what makes them timeless
3. Identify patterns (specificity, clarity, emotion)
4. Apply those patterns to modern contexts

**Timeless ≠ Old-Fashioned:**
- Timeless: Clear benefit, specific claim, emotional appeal
- Old-fashioned: Outdated language, irrelevant context

### 7.2 The Pride Test

**The Question:**
Are you proud enough of this work to show it to someone you respect?

**If the answer is "not really":**
- You know it's not good enough yet
- Keep rewriting until you're proud
- Better to ship less volume at higher quality

**The Professional Standard:**
Your name (or brand) is on every piece of copy. Protect that reputation with quality.

### 7.3 Continuous Improvement

**Practices:**
1. **Save Your Winners:** Keep a swipe file of best performers
2. **Analyze Losses:** When copy fails, understand why
3. **Study Masters:** Read classic books annually
4. **Test Relentlessly:** Data reveals truth
5. **Rewrite Everything:** First draft is never the best draft

## Part 8: Using This Document

### 8.1 For Writing (LLMs and Humans)

**Before writing:**
1. Read Principle 1-8 (refresh fundamentals)
2. Identify which principles apply most to this task
3. Note the constraints (word count, format, audience)

**During writing:**
1. Write expansively first (explore)
2. Apply principles during revision (sharpen)
3. Use Quality Rubric to score (assess)
4. Run Pre-Flight Checklist (validate)

**After writing:**
1. Comparative Test against competitors (beat them)
2. Read aloud (catch rhythm issues)
3. Show to someone cold (comprehension test)

### 8.2 For Evaluation

**When copy isn't working:**
1. Check each of the 8 principles—which is violated?
2. Run through Quality Rubric—which scores are below 4?
3. Apply relevant fixes from Part 4

**When copy IS working:**
1. Identify which principles made it work
2. Document the pattern
3. Apply to future projects

### 8.3 For Teaching

**Progressive Skill Building:**
1. Start with Principle 1 (Clarity)—master this first
2. Add Principle 2 (One Promise)—learn to focus
3. Layer in remaining principles gradually
4. Use Quality Rubric as grading tool

## Summary: The Eight Timeless Principles

1. **Clarity Beats Cleverness** - Always choose the clearer option
2. **One Strong Promise** - Every asset has ONE job
3. **Serve Self-Interest** - Lead with what they gain
4. **Be Concrete & Specific** - Numbers, names, comparisons
5. **Strong Verbs & Simple Words** - Active, everyday language
6. **Contrast & Surprise** - Create stopping power
7. **Visual-Copy Partnership** - 1+1=3 synergy
8. **Make Brand Clear** - Know who is speaking

**Master these eight, and you master the foundation of all great copy.**
